**AI Trust** is the degree of a user's tendency to trust an AI based on their personal assessment — a more emotional, psychological perspective. **AI Trustworthiness**, by contrast, is the degree of an AI's reliability as established by standardized, systematic testing that examines multiple layers and aspects of the system, such as reliability, safety, security, privacy, and accountability.

If the concepts of *AI trust* and *AI trustworthiness* are not fully distinguished, two major problems follow.

The first is **over-trust**, where people blindly believe an insecure AI system simply because of personal preference. In an over-trust situation, people can unconsciously leak confidential information and data to the system, which may lead to serious data-breach problems later.

The second is **under-trust**, where a secure and reliable AI system is rejected because it does not seem psychologically appealing and people lack confidence in it.

Both situations can cause serious conceptual confusion in research and policy-making. This can lead to measures that improve *public confidence* without improving the *system itself* — branding, pledges, or vague ethics statements — and to the mistaken belief that achieving trustworthiness is the same as achieving user trust, even though those are different goals requiring different interventions.

## What the standards say

The International Organization for Standardization addresses trustworthiness in AI through **ISO/IEC TR 24028:2020**, guidance on identifying and addressing potential vulnerabilities in AI systems. It surveys how to establish trust through transparency, explainability, and controllability, and discusses how to assess and achieve availability, resiliency, reliability, accuracy, safety, security, and privacy. It also notes engineering pitfalls, threats, risks, and mitigation methods.
